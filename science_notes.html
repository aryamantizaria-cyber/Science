What is a force?

F = m × a

A force is a push or pull acting on an object. Two types exist: contact and non-contact.

Contact forces

  • Friction — opposes motion when two surfaces rub
  • Tension — when an object is pulled from opposite ends
  • Normal contact force — when an object is supported by a surface

Non-contact forces

  • Electrostatic — between charged objects in an electric field (e.g. proton–electron attraction)
  • Magnetic — between poles in a magnetic field
  • Gravitational — between any two masses, acts at a distance

Friction

Opposes motion, slows objects down, and generates heat when excessive. Caused at the molecular level by surface irregularities pushing against each other.

Factors affecting friction

  • Rougher surfaces → more friction
  • More weight or pressure → more friction
  • More mass → more friction
Static

Acts when object is at rest. Prevents motion starting. Motion only begins when applied force exceeds static friction.

Sliding

Acts when surfaces slide over each other. Surface bonds break and reform. Opposes but cannot stop motion.

Rolling

Contact point keeps changing — much less friction than sliding. Example: adding wheels to suitcases.

Drag

Friction in fluids. Air resistance is a subtype — particles collide with the object, slowing it and causing heating.

Gravity

An attractive force between any two objects with mass. Works in a vacuum. Keeps us on Earth's surface.

Newton's law of universal gravitation

Fg = (G × m₁ × m₂) / d²
  • Fg = gravitational force (N)
  • G = gravitational constant (6.674 × 10⁻¹¹)
  • d = distance (m)  ·  m = mass (kg)
Always convert units before substituting into this formula.

Weight

W = m × g

Weight is the force of gravity, measured in Newtons using a spring balance. Varies by location — Earth g = 9.8 m/s², Moon g ≈ 1.6 m/s².

G (gravitational constant 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹) ≠ g (acceleration due to gravity, 9.8 m/s² on Earth).

Balanced and unbalanced forces

Resultant / net force — a single force describing the combined action of all forces on a body. Determines direction and magnitude of motion.

Balanced

Forces cancel out completely. Resultant force = 0. Object is in equilibrium — stays still or moves at constant velocity.

Unbalanced

Forces do not cancel. Non-zero resultant force. Object accelerates — Newton's 2nd law: F = ma.

Free fall

g = 9.8 m/s²

All objects accelerate toward Earth at 9.8 m/s². Without air resistance, any two objects dropped simultaneously hit the ground at the same time regardless of mass — Galileo demonstrated this at the Leaning Tower of Pisa.

The atmosphere creates an upward drag force. Larger surface area → more drag → slower fall. Terminal velocity is reached when drag equals gravitational force — the object falls at constant speed. Skydivers use spread-eagle position to increase drag and lower terminal velocity.

Properties of magnets

  • Have magnetic fields around them
  • Two poles — like poles repel, opposite poles attract
  • Exert little force on non-magnetic materials
  • Can induce magnetism in magnetic materials

Magnetic vs non-magnetic metals

Magnetic Iron · Nickel · Cobalt

Non-magnetic Copper · Tin · Zinc · Aluminium · Brass

In an unmagnetized material, atomic magnets point randomly. When magnetized, they align in one direction and act as one large magnet. Demagnetization: strike hard or heat to high temperature.

Hard ferromagnets

Permanent. Difficult to magnetize but retain magnetism. Used in microphones, compasses, fridge seals.

Soft ferromagnets

Temporary. Easy to magnetize but lose magnetism quickly. Used in electromagnets and transformers.

Magnetic fields

Field lines run from south to north pole. Strongest where lines are closest. Unlike poles facing each other combine into a uniform field. Like poles create a neutral point where field strength = 0.

To magnetize a steel bar: place in a solenoid coil, pass large current. More current or more turns = stronger magnet.

Electromagnets

A magnet whose field is produced by electric current. Can be switched on and off. A solenoid is a long coil of wire — current through it produces a much stronger field than a straight wire.

Increasing field strength

  • Increase the current
  • Increase the number of turns in the coil
  • Use a soft iron core

Uses

Scrapyard crane

Current on → core magnetizes → picks up scrap. Current off → core demagnetizes → releases scrap instantly.

Electric bell

Push switch closes circuit → coil becomes electromagnet → attracts armature → hammer hits gong → contact switch breaks → electromagnet off → spring pulls armature back → cycle repeats.

Relay

Uses low-current circuit to control a high-current circuit via an electromagnet. Found in car starter motors — safe because you never directly switch the high-current side.

DC motor

Converts electrical energy into rotational mechanical energy. A commutator reverses current every half-turn to maintain continuous rotation in one direction.

The nucleus and nuclear forces

Located at the center of the atom. Contains protons (+) and neutrons (neutral). Nucleons = collective term for protons and neutrons. Nuclear forces hold nucleons together — short range, essentially zero beyond 10⁻¹² cm.

Fission

Nuclear fission

Heavy unstable nucleus splits into two lighter nuclei, releasing energy + neutrons. Neutrons hit other nuclei → chain reaction. Used in nuclear power plants. Uranium-235 is the most common fuel (~200 MeV per event).

Fusion

Nuclear fusion

Two light nuclei combine into one heavier nucleus, releasing even more energy. Requires temperatures above 100 million °C. Occurs in stars. Produces 3–4× more energy per unit mass than fission. E = mc².

Chernobyl (1986)

Nuclear plants use controlled fission: uranium splits → heat → steam → turbines → electricity.

Control methods

  • Control rods — absorb neutrons. More rods = slower reaction, fewer = faster.
  • Coolant — water absorbs heat, prevents overheating
  • Containment structure — thick concrete and steel prevents radiation leakage

Causes

  • Design flaw: no proper containment structure
  • Human error: control rods mishandled and removed

Effects

  • 2 deaths from explosion · 29 from acute radiation syndrome
  • Thousands of thyroid cancer cases in children, even decades later, from radioactive iodine

Satellites

Artificial

Man-made. Powered by solar cells. Communicate via radio waves and antennas.

Natural

Celestial bodies like moons. Held in orbit by gravity. Formed from post-planetary debris over millions of years.

Applications of artificial satellites

  • Communication — TV, telephone, internet relay
  • Remote sensing — weather monitoring, storm tracking, military surveillance
  • Navigation — GPS positioning data
  • Astronomy — space telescopes like Hubble, free from atmospheric interference

The big bang theory

All matter and energy began compressed into a singularity — a single, extremely hot and dense point. It exploded approximately 13.8 billion years ago, producing the universe.

Evidence

  • Cosmic microwave background (CMB) — faint radiation detected throughout all of space. The afterglow of the Big Bang, confirming the universe was once very hot and has been cooling ever since.
  • Red shift — light from distant galaxies always shifts toward the red (long-wavelength) end of the spectrum because galaxies are constantly moving apart.

Hubble's law

Galaxies move apart from each other, and their recessional velocity is proportional to their distance. The further a galaxy is, the faster it recedes.

Universe estimated at 13.8 billion years old based on expansion rate and cosmic background radiation.

Electrostatic force

The force between any two charged particles. Charge is measured in coulombs (C).

  • Unlike charges → attract
  • Like charges → repel

Real-life example — Van de Graaff generator

Charge passes from the dome to a person's hair. All hairs become negatively charged. Like charges repel — the repulsion force is strong enough to make hair stand up. An electric field then surrounds both the dome and the person.

Electrostatic force follows an inverse-square law — it weakens as distance increases, just like gravity.

Carbon

Group 14 · Period 2 Atomic number 6 Valency = 4

Carbon shares 4 electrons to reach stability, forming covalent bonds. It does not form ionic bonds — losing 4 electrons requires too much energy, gaining 4 makes it unstable.

In free state, carbon exists only as: Diamond, Graphite, or Fullerene.

Catenation

Bonds with other carbon atoms to form long chains or rings. Examples: methane, ethane, benzene.

Multiple bonding

Forms single, double, and triple bonds. Ethane (single), Ethene (double), Ethyne (triple).

Tetravalency

Can form four covalent bonds with other atoms, allowing enormous structural variety.

Organic vs inorganic compounds

Organic — primarily made of carbon-hydrogen bonds. Examples: alcohol, methane, glucose.

Inorganic — do not contain carbon-hydrogen bonds. Examples: water, carbon dioxide.

Eutrophication

Enrichment of water bodies with inorganic nutrients, mainly nitrate (NO₃⁻) and phosphate (PO₄³⁻), from fertilizers, sewage, and industrial discharge.

Process

  1. Excess nutrients enter water through runoff
  2. Nutrients stimulate rapid algal growth (algal bloom)
  3. Large amounts of organic biomass accumulate
  4. Microorganisms decompose biomass, consuming dissolved oxygen
  5. Hypoxic conditions form → fish deaths → dead zones

Case study: Lake Erie

Excessive phosphorus and nitrogen since the mid-20th century have accelerated eutrophication, threatening drinking water, fisheries, and ecosystem stability. Climate warming intensifies the process by increasing algal growth rates.

Controlling agricultural fertilizer use

Advantage

Reduces nitrogen and phosphorus entering the lake. Decreases algal blooms, improves water quality, restores aquatic ecosystems, reduces dead zones. Targets the problem at its source.

Limitation

Difficult to monitor across many farms. Farmers depend on fertilizers for yield — strict regulations affect income. Nutrients already in soil and sediments continue entering the lake for years, so improvements are not immediate.

Conclusion

Effective as a long-term solution but must be combined with farmer education, sustainable farming practices, and monitoring programs.

Seed germination

The process by which a seed grows into a young plant (seedling).

Stages

  1. Imbibition — seed rapidly absorbs water, coat swells and softens
  2. Lag phase — enzymes activate, respiration begins, proteins produced, stored food metabolized
  3. Emergence — coat ruptures, radicle forms roots, plumule emerges, shoot grows upward
Water
  • Softens seed coat during imbibition
  • Provides dissolved oxygen to embryo
Oxygen
  • Required for metabolism and respiration until leaves develop
  • Found in pores of soil particles
Temperature

Optimal range: 25–30°C for most seeds.

Sunlight

Most seeds require sunlight. Some specialized seeds germinate only in darkness.

Gas exchange in alveoli

Occurs by diffusion along concentration gradients. O₂ diffuses from alveoli into blood. CO₂ diffuses from blood into alveoli to be exhaled.

Role of cartilage

  • Structural support — C-shaped rings prevent trachea and bronchi collapsing
  • Protection — flexible but strong, handles pressure changes during coughing without damage

Diseases

Asthma

Inflammation and narrowing of airways → difficulty breathing.

Bronchitis

Inflamed bronchi from infection or smoking. Excess mucus, narrowed airways.

Pneumonia

Lung infection → fluid in alveoli → impaired gas exchange.

Emphysema

Alveoli damage reduces gas exchange efficiency. Common in smokers.

Respiration

A series of chemical reactions releasing energy inside every living cell.

Aerobic

In mitochondria. Requires oxygen. Produces CO₂, water, and large amounts of ATP. Slow but sustained.

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP
Anaerobic

In cytoplasm. No oxygen needed. Small amounts of ATP. Fast but cannot be sustained.

Animals → lactic acid + energy

Yeast/plants → ethanol + CO₂ + energy

Fermentation

A type of anaerobic respiration — microorganisms break down sugars without oxygen.

Alcoholic

Yeast and some bacteria. Glucose → ethanol + CO₂ + energy. Used in brewing, baking, winemaking. CO₂ causes bread to rise.

C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂
Lactic acid

Bacteria and human muscle cells. Glucose → lactic acid + energy. Used in yogurt and cheese. Causes muscle fatigue during intense exercise.

C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₃H₆O₃

Uses of fermentation

Food industry (bread, yogurt, cheese) · Alcohol · Biofuels · Pharmaceuticals

Classification of living organisms

Accurately names organisms, prevents misnomers, uses a shared Latinized language, and reveals interrelationships.

Taxonomy hierarchy

Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

Binomial nomenclature

Two words: Genus (capital) + species (lowercase). Both italicized or underlined. Both Latinized. Example: Homo sapiens, Pisum sativum.

R.H. Whittaker's 5 kingdoms

Monera

Prokaryotic, unicellular, auto- or heterotrophic. Example: Bacteria.

Protista

Eukaryotic, unicellular, auto- or heterotrophic. Example: Amoeba.

Fungi

Eukaryotic, saprophytic, have cell walls. Some symbiotic with algae. Example: Mushroom.

Plantae

Eukaryotic, multicellular, autotrophic, have cell walls. Example: Rose.

Animalia

Eukaryotic, multicellular, heterotrophic, no cell wall. Example: Humans.

Indoor air pollution

Arises from various indoor sources. Significantly impacts health, especially in vulnerable populations.

Sources

  • Household fuels and cooking
  • Biological contaminants — mould, dust mites, pet dander
  • Combustion appliances — furnaces and gas stoves emit CO and NO₂
  • Household cleaners and personal care products

Health impacts

  • Respiratory diseases — asthma, COPD
  • Cardiovascular problems — heart attack and stroke
  • Cognitive deficits and developmental issues in young children
  • Increased cancer risk

Vulnerable populations

Children and infants · Senior citizens · Low-income households with poor ventilation · People with pre-existing conditions
